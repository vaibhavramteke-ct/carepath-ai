# 1. Patient Journey Overview

A patient journey is the complete experience a patient goes through when interacting with a hospital, clinic, diagnostic center, doctor, pharmacy, insurance team, or care team.

It usually starts when the patient notices a symptom or medical need and continues through doctor discovery, appointment booking, consultation, tests, treatment, billing, discharge, recovery, follow-up, and long-term health engagement.

## Key Phases

1. **Need identification**
   * Patient feels symptoms or needs medical help.

2. **Care discovery**
   * Patient searches for the right doctor, department, hospital, or service.

3. **Access and appointment**
   * Patient books appointment, prepares documents, understands cost and location.

4. **Visit and consultation**
   * Patient arrives, checks in, consults doctor, receives advice.

5. **Diagnostics and treatment**
   * Tests, scans, procedures, medications, surgery, or admission may happen.

6. **Billing and insurance**
   * Patient handles payments, estimates, approvals, reimbursement, or cashless claims.

7. **Discharge and recovery**
   * Patient receives discharge summary, medicines, care instructions, warning signs.

8. **Follow-up and long-term engagement**
   * Patient continues medication, tracks recovery, books follow-ups, manages chronic care, and receives preventive health support.

## Stage 1: Symptom Discovery / Need Identification

### Patient goal

Understand whether the symptom needs medical attention and what to do next.

### Patient emotions or concerns

* Anxiety
* Confusion
* Fear of serious illness
* Uncertainty about urgency
* Worry about cost or hospital visit

### Common patient actions

* Searches symptoms online
* Asks family or friends
* Calls hospital helpline
* Looks for nearby doctors
* Tries home remedies
* Waits to see if symptoms improve

### Hospital/provider touchpoints

* Website
* Mobile app
* Helpline
* AI chatbot
* Emergency number
* Primary care desk

### Information the patient may need

* Which department to consult
* Whether symptoms are urgent
* Basic home care guidance
* Emergency warning signs
* Available doctors
* Appointment options

### Common pain points

* Online symptom information creates panic
* Patient does not know which doctor to visit
* Long call waiting time
* Confusion between emergency, OPD, and teleconsultation
* Language barriers

### AI chatbot opportunities

* Safe symptom triage with disclaimers
* Suggest department, not final diagnosis
* Identify emergency red flags
* Recommend appointment or ER escalation
* Provide simple-language guidance
* Support local languages

### Example chatbot queries

1. “I have fever and body pain since yesterday. Which doctor should I consult?”
2. “Is chest pain an emergency?”
3. “My child has vomiting. Should I come to the hospital now?”
4. “I am feeling dizzy. Can I wait until tomorrow?”
5. “Mujhe pet mein dard hai, kaunse doctor ko dikhana chahiye?”
6. “My father is 72 and has breathing difficulty. What should I do?”
7. “Can I consult a doctor online for cold and cough?”
8. “I have severe headache and blurred vision. Is it serious?”
9. “Is there an emergency department open now?”

### 2. Doctor and Department Search

# 7. Safety and Compliance Considerations

Healthcare AI must be designed with strong safety controls.

## Important Safety Rules

### 1. Do not provide final diagnosis

The chatbot should never say:

> “You have pneumonia.”

Instead, it should say:

> “Your symptoms may need medical evaluation. Please consult a doctor for diagnosis.”

***

### 2. Clearly separate general guidance from medical advice

Use language like:

> “This is general information and not a substitute for professional medical advice.”

***

### 3. Escalate emergency symptoms immediately

The bot should detect and escalate symptoms such as:

* Chest pain
* Difficulty breathing
* Stroke symptoms
* Severe bleeding
* Loss of consciousness
* Seizures
* Severe allergic reaction
* Suicidal thoughts or self-harm risk
* Severe abdominal pain in pregnancy
* High fever in infants
* Sudden weakness or confusion

The bot should respond with:

> “This may be urgent. Please seek emergency medical care immediately or call local emergency services.”

***

### 4. Protect patient privacy

The chatbot should avoid exposing:

* Diagnosis
* Reports
* Prescriptions
* Insurance details
* Payment details
* Personal identifiers

without authentication.

***

### 5. Use authentication for sensitive data

For personal information, the bot should require:

* Login
* OTP
* Member ID verification
* Date of birth verification
* Role-based access for caregivers

***

### 6. Maintain audit logs

Keep logs for:

* User query
* Bot response
* Escalation decision
* Handoff to human
* Clinical content shown
* Consent capture
* Access to sensitive data

***

### 7. Human handoff is mandatory

The bot should escalate when:

* Patient asks for diagnosis
* Symptoms are urgent
* Patient is confused about medication safety
* Insurance or billing dispute occurs
* Patient is angry or distressed
* Bot confidence is low

***

### 8. Avoid unsafe medication advice

The bot should not independently change:

* Medicine dosage
* Treatment duration
* Antibiotic usage
* Insulin dosage
* Blood thinner instructions

Instead:

> “Please confirm this with your doctor or pharmacist before making any medication change.”

***

### 9. Support consent and caregiver access

Caregiver access should be permission-based.

Example:

> “Vaibhav has allowed you to view appointment reminders and medicine schedule, but not billing details.”

***

### 10. Use approved medical content

Clinical responses should come from:

* Doctor-approved FAQs
* Hospital care protocols
* Validated discharge templates
* Approved patient education material
